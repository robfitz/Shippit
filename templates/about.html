A quick half decade

About 5 years ago I dropped out of grad school to start a game studio. We got sidetracked and spent 4 years making advertising technology, with the kind support of ycombinator (summer '07) and a wonderful team of investors & advisors in SF and London.

So I'm going to make these games

Flash forward to today (skipping over one or two more failures and experiments) and I'm back to games. Working on what you love is a real competitive advantage, and I've got a vision for a new generation of collectible card games, designed for competitive online tournament play, and embracing every player as a game designer.

But first, I'm making some tools

So I began making those games, and started to see a couple problems, which means it's time to put the games on hold for a couple weeks and go problem solving.

First, everyone involved in games has a shitty portfolio (except artists). Managing a portfolio should be a non-issue, especially when the output is visually rich and online anyway. Github has helped a foster a ton of developer collaboration, and we can do the same for games.

Second, a lot of promising projects never ship. This is borderline tragic. You've got brilliant, passionate people, and they lose steam or are missing a particular skillset or bit of encouragement and, as a result, the world never gets their games.

Third, a team of hobbyists finds it tough to easily collaborate and fairly compensate themselves (or for a team of professionals to deal with loosely involved contributers). A huge number of potential game creators have other, full-time lives. I think it's possible to allow all these groups to work together fluidly, and to be rewarded for their contributions. I won't dwell on this now, since it's the furthest off, but it's what I'm most excited about.

Blah blah blah what have you actually done?

Yeah yeah I know, promises are in ample supply. So today I've launched the start of an open game development community. The idea is that we can all share our development process and build a portfolio and reputation for shipping. That helps us know who is worth collaboarating with (ie. they ship finished projects instead of flaking out at 80% complete).

I'm rolling it out on an invitation basis, since a lot of the behind-the-scenes stuff (e.g. registration, account management, creating new posts) is still pretty ugly, and I don't want to shock any chillins with the sight of a raw admin interface. If you'd like an invite, shoot me a quick email with a couple words (exactly 2) about what you're working on and how you'd like to use this sort of community.

Also, I'm collaborating with a couple brilliant guys on a fair collaboration and compensation model for this sort of project. I hope to be releasing a whitepaper soon, grounding it in the traditions of gainsharing and startup founder equity. I'm trialling the model with my current team, involving myself and 3 other very-part-time helpers who are working around their own full-time jobs and startups.
